The Prompt

The Situation

Two weeks ago in Austin, Texas, a Waymo car hesitated for 14 seconds at an unprotected left turn. No safety issue occurred — but the rider gave it a 2-star review and tweeted. Engineering concluded it was both a perception problem and a planner problem. The VP of Strategy asked me to figure out what that meant, and to recommend which city Waymo should launch in next.

That single incident became the thread connecting six analytical steps: from the mechanics of how neural networks actually learn, to the economics of training data, to the design of reward functions, to the politics of urban expansion.

"A 14-second hesitation is a product failure, a PR problem, and a strategy signal. The question isn't just how to fix the planner — it's what the hesitation tells us about where the model was trained, what it's never seen, and where we go next."
01

Foundation

Deep Learning Basics

I began by training a small neural network by hand — labeling images as "cat" or "not cat" and watching the network's feature weights adjust with each example. The exercise made concrete something that's easy to gloss over: a neural network isn't programmed with rules; it builds its own internal representation of the world from labeled evidence, iteratively. The more I labeled, the better it got. The moment I stopped labeling things it had never seen, it was on its own.

From there, I studied how convolutional neural networks process visual input in layers — raw pixels become edges, edges become shapes, shapes become objects. The final stage of this pipeline for Waymo is answering the question: is that a pedestrian? But the exercise that most changed my thinking was the long-tail frequency chart. Common scenarios — sunny highways, empty intersections — make up the vast majority of real-world driving miles. The scenarios that actually cause failures (costumed children, wheelchair users, murals on trucks, mattresses on the freeway) cluster at the far right of the distribution, each individually rare but collectively dangerous. A model is only as good as the edge cases in its training data. That problem doesn't solve itself at scale — it requires deliberate strategy.

No Claude artifact for this step — foundational concepts only.

Key concepts: labeled learning, CNN layer architecture, the long-tail problem.

02

Data Acquisition

Deep Learning at Waymo

I played the role of a Waymo ML analyst evaluating three training-dataset vendors: Dataset A ("Easy Miles" — sunny Phoenix daylight frames, $100M), Dataset B ("Balanced" — varied conditions, $180M), and Dataset C ("Edge-Case Heavy" — rare scenarios weighted, $300M). I trained a simulated perception network on each and measured accuracy across both routine and edge-case test scenes.

Dataset A produced a 33% overall accuracy — a disqualifying result driven by how narrow its training distribution was. Dataset C achieved 88% accuracy on edge-case scenarios, the exact scenarios that determine safety outcomes in the real world. The tradeoff I accepted: a modest accuracy dip on routine favorable-condition driving, which is both the easiest problem and the one where failure costs the least. I drafted the following email to the CFO defending that choice.

Artifact — CFO Email: Dataset Recommendation

Subject: Training Data Recommendation: Dataset C (Edge-Case Heavy) Hi [CFO name], I'm recommending we proceed with Dataset C at $300M. Three reasons: 1. Safety profile. Dataset C achieves 88% accuracy on rare, high-stakes scenarios — the exact situations where AV failures cause harm and headlines. Dataset A's 33% overall accuracy is disqualifying at our safety bar. 2. Regulatory positioning. NHTSA and international regulators are increasingly scrutinizing edge-case performance. A model that handles adverse conditions is an asset in certification conversations, not a liability. 3. Competitive moat. Waymo's differentiation is operating in conditions competitors avoid. Dataset C trains directly to that thesis. The tradeoff I'm accepting: a likely small accuracy dip on routine, favorable-condition driving — the scenario where all competitors perform well and where the cost of imperfection is lowest. 03

Simulation Strategy

World Models

Dataset C addresses the long tail better than the alternatives — but it's still limited by what someone has actually recorded. Step 3 was about understanding how Waymo escapes that constraint entirely. The world model is an AI trained on reality that can generate driving scenarios that have never been captured on film, by composing ingredients it understands separately: weather conditions, time of day, road actors, and wildcard events. I built several such scenarios in the simulator — including a rainy night with children near the road and a mattress blocking the lane — and compared how many real-world miles it would take to encounter each naturally. For that specific scenario: approximately 168 million miles.

I then turned the analysis strategic: Tesla collects data passively from six million cars, which is an enormous volume advantage at the center of the driving distribution. But volume doesn't solve the tail — a billion sunny-day miles in Phoenix don't teach a model what to do the first time it snows there. Targeted generation does. I drafted the following internal brief arguing that world model investment is a structural moat, not an R&D cost center.

Artifact — Internal Brief: World Model as Strategic Moat

TO: VP of Strategy FROM: Strategy Analyst RE: World Model Simulation — Strategic Moat, Not R&D Overhead Our world model simulation capacity does something no fleet can: manufacture rare reality on demand. Fleet driving is a volume strategy. At scale, it captures what happens frequently. But the scenarios that determine safety outcomes — and regulatory trust — are precisely those that happen almost never. A single composite scenario we built last week (rainy night, children near the road, mattress in the lane) would require an estimated 168 million real-world miles to encounter naturally. Our simulator generated it in seconds by composing conditions it already understood separately. On Tesla: their fleet data advantage is real and shouldn't be dismissed. But volume solves the center of the distribution, not the tail. A billion sunny-day miles do not teach a model what to do the first time it snows in Phoenix. Targeted generation is structurally different from scale — it's the only way to cover scenarios the real world delivers too rarely to learn from. If we deprioritize simulation investment, we don't slow down — we regress. Competitors close the gap on common conditions quickly. The long tail is where our safety record and our regulatory positioning actually live. That's what we'd be giving up.

04

Planner Design

Planning with Reinforcement Learning

This was the most technically direct step — and the one that made the Austin incident concrete. I designed a reward function to teach Waymo's planner how to handle an unprotected left turn. The simulator gave me four sliders: one reward for completing a turn (+4 points) and three penalties for collisions (−10), close calls (−5), and wait time per second (−5), with a hard budget of 20 penalty points total. Every allocation was a tradeoff: weight collisions too heavily and the policy learns paralysis; weight wait time too heavily and it learns aggression. The budget forces you to be explicit about what you value.

My 300-attempt test results: 206 successful turns (target: ≥285), 1 collision (target: ≤1), 9.8 seconds average wait (target: <8). I met the collision bar. I missed completions and wait time — a direct consequence of the cautious allocation I accepted. The safety board defense below makes that tradeoff explicit, because a safety board will find it regardless.

Artifact — Safety Board Defense: Reward Function

This reward function prioritized collision avoidance above all else, assigning 10 of 20 penalty points to collisions and splitting the remaining budget evenly between close calls (5) and hesitation (5) — a deliberate choice to treat physical safety as the hard constraint and throughput as secondary. On that primary bar, the policy succeeded: 1 collision across 300 attempts meets the ≤1 ceiling. However, two bars were not met. Successful completions reached 206 of 300 (68.7%), well below the ≥285 target, and average wait time came in at 9.8 seconds against a <8 second requirement. The tradeoff I explicitly accepted was over-weighting the wait penalty relative to the completion reward — a +4 completion bonus against a 5-point/second wait penalty created a policy that learned hesitation as a loss-minimization strategy rather than as a failure mode. The next iteration will rebalance the completion reward upward to incentivize throughput without relaxing the collision ceiling.

05

Business Strategy

Data Strategy & The AI Business

Having spent four steps on the technical side, Step 5 shifted the frame to business strategy. I studied the contrast between Waymo's simulation-first approach and Tesla's fleet-first approach through three diagnostic questions: Where does the data come from? Does the pipeline compound — does it keep improving on its own? Can a competitor replicate it with capital alone? These questions cut through the noise of AI company marketing and get at whether a data advantage is structural or temporary.

To test whether I'd internalized the framework, I applied the three questions to a different AI company — Duolingo — chosen because its data moat comes from behavioral language-learning signals rather than any technical infrastructure, which requires a different kind of reasoning to evaluate.

Artifact — Data Moat Evaluation: Duolingo

1. Where does Duolingo's data come from? Duolingo's data comes from 500M+ registered users generating real interaction signals every session — not just right/wrong answers, but hesitation time, retry patterns, streak breaks, and which hints get tapped. This is behavioral language-learning data at a scale no academic corpus or licensing deal can replicate, because it captures how humans actually struggle with a language, not just what correct language looks like. 2. Does the pipeline compound? Yes, and meaningfully so. Every learner who completes a lesson generates labeled data about what explanation style worked, what exercise format caused drop-off, and what spaced-repetition interval produced retention — that feedback continuously retrains their difficulty and pacing models without Duolingo writing a single new line of curriculum. The compounding mechanism is the product loop itself: more users → better personalization → better retention → more users. 3. Can a competitor replicate it with $2B? A competitor can buy GPT-4 access, hire curriculum designers, and build a polished app — Babbel and Busuu have done versions of this. What they can't buy is 15 years of interaction data tied to learning outcomes across hundreds of languages and skill levels, nor the A/B testing history that taught Duolingo which nudges actually change behavior. Verdict: Strong moat — their data is proprietary, behavioral rather than textual, and self-reinforcing through the product loop in a way that scales faster than any competitor starting from scratch today.

06

Final Recommendation

The City Launch Decision

The final step was synthesis. I analyzed three real-world Waymo social-license incidents — protest "coning" in San Francisco, a blocked fire truck in Austin, taxi-union opposition in Miami — and developed a response framework. I then evaluated four candidate cities (Miami, Seattle, Atlanta, New York) against what I'd established across Steps 1–5: perception stack readiness, world model coverage gaps, planner behavior fit, data moat growth potential, and social-license risk. My recommendation was Seattle.

Seattle is demanding in the right ways. Constant rain, steep hills, and high-density micro-mobility environments stress exactly the perception and planning capabilities that distinguish Waymo from competitors. The data generated there is irreplaceable. The social-license environment is organized but navigable — unlike Atlanta (active NHTSA investigation) or New York (TWU + Taxi Alliance already blocked statewide expansion). The memo below was written for the VP of Strategy.

Artifact — Recommendation Memo: Seattle as City #5

TO: VP of Strategy | RE: City #5 Launch Recommendation: Seattle, WA Recommendation Launch Seattle as Waymo's fifth market. Seattle's risk profile is demanding — it is the right kind of demanding. The city's permanent rain, steep topography, and high-density micro-mobility environment will stress the perception stack and planner in ways Phoenix, San Francisco, and Austin have not. Three Reasons 1. Tech Readiness. Dataset C's 88% edge-case accuracy reflects a model prepared for non-standard perception environments. The world model pre-generates 50,000+ Seattle-specific synthetic scenarios — black ice on a 15% grade, cyclists in downpours, pedestrians darting between buses in Belltown — before the first vehicle goes live. 2. Data Moat. The moat Seattle builds is weather-edge-case depth: validated rain-night-hill frames no competitor in sunbelt markets can replicate. Simulation coverage gap identification. WSDOT/Seattle DOT regulatory signal.

Transferable Frameworks

What I Can Now Apply to Any AI Company

The Long-Tail Problem Common data is cheap; tail data is what determines real-world performance. Any AI company's failure modes live in the scenarios they haven't trained on.
World Models & Transfer Learning The most powerful data strategies generate scenarios rather than collect them — composing from learned physics rather than waiting for the real world to cooperate.
The Alignment Problem Reward functions encode values. Whatever you penalize most is what the system learns to fear. The Austin hesitation was a reward function that had over-learned caution.
Compounding Data Moats The best AI data pipelines improve themselves: more users → better model → better product → more users. One-shot datasets don't compound; behavioral feedback loops do.
The Prediction–Decision Gap Perception accuracy and planner behavior are separate problems. A model can see the pedestrian and still hesitate. Accuracy metrics don't capture decision quality.
Social License as Strategy Technical performance is necessary but not sufficient. The cities and regulators that allow deployment determine the scope of any AI company's data advantage.
